Why I'm giving up Obsidian

Posted on 2023-12-25

Obsidian is indeed a very powerful tool that integrates many amazing features like graphs, backlinks, hashtags. As a note-taking software it also has a large user base that almost guarantees its success. This active community has created a variety of tutorials for almost everything, coded plugins and provided feedbacks for the obsidian official to constantly improve toward the ultimate the solution of writing app.

Yet it won’t be the solution for me personally, because it contradicts my philosophy of notes and is a real time-sucker.

The unresolvable conflict

The good notes should always be the essence of anything, in my opinion, or the reductionist view. Almost everything else would be examples, explanations, extensions of that very core notion. It has become very easy that our “course notes” comprise of hundreds of definitons, examples, remarks, stuff like that. But these notes, whether taken electronically or physically, will not eventually stay in your mind intact. All that left with you as you begin your final exam will only be the most important ideas, no matter it’s your understanding, cases you have rememberd or the several key formulas. Honestly, that’s all that counts. In other words, the best notes shall look like some cheatsheet created on our own.

Obsidian is not an app designed that. As a all-in-one software, it has many features I won’t use and think of to use. Take one of the selling points, the backlinks, for example. It is cool, admittedly, that you can create a graph connecting all the dots you have marked out. But what’s the point of solely being cool? It will neither help you remember more concepts nor shed light on any of the obscure formulas and codes. Obsidian has many other features so similiar to that, resulting in such a huge and bloated software. No wonder it can satisfy almost everyone’s needs, and some features are provided as “core plugins” and can be manually turned off. Tho it’s just too advanced an app for me.

The configurability and playability

As one of the most popular note-taking apps, Obsidian has a considerable base of users, many of whom has experience in front-end development. Therefore there are tons of community plugins that you can use with merely a tap on the install.

The community has come up with solutions for different demands. You add .vimrc to enhance vim mode/quickly sync your contents across platforms/get differnet elegant themes all by browsing the plugin community. It is a key to a thriving software, to have enough users and creators that would contribute for making the software more capable.

So I guess the reason I quit would be quite identical to the ones I relied less and less on neovim. The point of these tools is not to play with, but to finish the task. To sort out thoughts, to create meaningful contents, to build strong and high-quality software rather than merely play with the tools. Some take that as a hobby and evidently I’m not one of them.

In short

Don’t get me wrong if you have. Obsidian is an amazing app that could almost handles every circumstances. The only downside might be that its so time-consuming. Instead of focusing on actual work, it tends to tempt you into trying out all sorts of new things, which is kinda counter to why the app was made in the first place.
